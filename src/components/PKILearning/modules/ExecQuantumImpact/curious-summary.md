# The Quantum Threat — What Business Leaders Need to Know

## What This Is About

If you run a business or sit on a board, here is the short version: the encryption that protects your company's data, customer records, financial transactions, and intellectual property has an expiration date. Quantum computers — a fundamentally new kind of computing technology — will eventually be powerful enough to break the math that current encryption relies on.

Think of your company's data protection like a safe with a combination lock. Today, the combination is so complex that cracking it would take millions of years. Quantum computers change the equation. They could find the combination in hours or days. The safe itself is fine, but you need a new lock — and swapping it out across an entire organization takes years of planning, budgeting, and execution.

## Why It Matters

This is not just an IT problem. It is a business risk that affects legal compliance, customer trust, competitive advantage, and potentially even your company's survival. Regulations in the United States, European Union, and Asia are already beginning to require quantum-readiness planning. Companies that handle sensitive data — financial records, health information, government contracts, trade secrets — face the greatest urgency.

There is also a hidden threat called "harvest now, decrypt later." Adversaries are already collecting encrypted data today, stockpiling it until quantum computers can break it open. If your company's data has long-term value — and most companies' data does — the clock is already ticking.

## The Key Takeaway

The transition to quantum-safe security is a multi-year, organization-wide effort that requires executive sponsorship, dedicated budget, and cross-functional coordination. Waiting for quantum computers to arrive before acting means starting too late.

## What's Happening

Governments worldwide are setting deadlines. The United States has mandated that federal agencies begin transitioning by 2025, with completion targets around 2035. The EU's NIS2 directive and sector-specific regulations are incorporating quantum risk. NIST has published the first quantum-resistant encryption standards. Major consulting firms, industry groups, and technology vendors are offering quantum-readiness assessments and migration roadmaps. Forward-thinking companies are already conducting cryptographic inventories — cataloging every place encryption is used — as the essential first step toward upgrading.
