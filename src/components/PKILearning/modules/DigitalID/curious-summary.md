# Digital ID — In Simple Terms

## What This Is About

A digital ID is the electronic version of the cards in your wallet — your driver's license, passport, or insurance card — stored securely on your phone or computer. Instead of handing a physical card to someone, you can share a verified digital version. Think of it like having a notarized copy of your ID that lives on your phone and can prove who you are without revealing more information than necessary. For example, you could prove you are over 21 without sharing your exact birthday or home address.

Behind the scenes, digital IDs rely on cryptographic signatures — mathematical seals that prove the ID was issued by a trusted authority (like a government) and has not been altered. These signatures currently use encryption methods that quantum computers could eventually forge. If that happens, someone could create a fake digital ID that looks perfectly legitimate, just as if someone could perfectly counterfeit a government-issued driver's license.

## Why It Matters

Digital IDs are quickly becoming part of daily life. The European Union is rolling out digital identity wallets for all citizens. U.S. states are launching mobile driver's licenses. Airlines, banks, and healthcare systems are beginning to accept digital credentials. As more of our identity verification moves online, the stakes of getting security wrong grow enormously.

If the cryptographic signatures behind digital IDs are not upgraded to resist quantum computers, the entire system of trust could collapse. Someone with access to a powerful quantum computer could forge identity documents, impersonate others, or undermine election systems that rely on digital verification. The consequences would extend far beyond any single person — they could shake public trust in digital government services altogether.

## The Key Takeaway

Digital IDs depend on cryptographic signatures to prove they are real. Those signatures must be upgraded to quantum-safe methods before quantum computers can forge them, or the entire digital identity system — from driver's licenses to passports to voting credentials — could be undermined.

## What's Happening

The European Union's eIDAS 2.0 regulation, which establishes digital identity wallets for EU citizens, is actively considering quantum-safe requirements. The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) is studying how to protect electronic passports. ISO standards for mobile driver's licenses are being updated. Several countries, including South Korea, Singapore, and Germany, are running pilot programs that test quantum-safe digital ID systems. The race is on to upgrade these systems before they become targets.
