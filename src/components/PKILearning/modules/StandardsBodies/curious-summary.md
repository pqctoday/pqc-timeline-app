# Standards, Certification & Compliance Bodies — In Simple Terms

## What This Is About

When you buy a fire extinguisher, you trust that it will actually work in an emergency. That trust does not come from the manufacturer's promise alone — it comes from independent organizations that tested the extinguisher, certified it meets safety standards, and required that every unit sold meets those same rules. The world of encryption works the same way.

There are three types of organizations involved. First, standards bodies write the rulebooks — they define what "secure encryption" means and publish the technical specifications. Second, certification labs test products against those rulebooks to verify they actually work as claimed. Third, compliance authorities are the enforcers — they are the government agencies and regulators that say "you must use certified encryption by this date, or face consequences."

For example, NIST (in the U.S.) both writes standards and runs certification programs. ISO (international) creates global standards. ETSI (Europe) writes telecom and internet standards. And agencies like the NSA or Europe's ANSSI set deadlines and requirements for when organizations must upgrade.

## Why It Matters

Without this system, anyone could claim their encryption is "quantum-safe" without proof. Companies could sell products that look secure but have never been properly tested. Governments could not set consistent requirements. The whole ecosystem of trust — from online banking to national defense — would fall apart.

For organizations planning their transition to quantum-safe encryption, understanding who sets the standards, who tests the products, and who enforces the deadlines is essential. Choosing a product that is not certified by the right body could mean failing an audit, losing a contract, or being non-compliant with the law.

## The Key Takeaway

Secure encryption is not just about math — it is about a system of trust. Standards bodies write the rules, certification labs verify the products, and compliance authorities enforce the deadlines. Understanding this ecosystem is essential for making smart decisions about the quantum-safe transition.

## What's Happening

NIST published its first post-quantum encryption standards in 2024, and certification of products against those standards is underway. Europe is aligning through ETSI and ENISA, with country-level agencies like BSI (Germany) and ANSSI (France) issuing their own guidance. International coordination is happening through ISO and the Common Criteria framework. The result is a global, interconnected system where standards developed in one country are recognized and adopted by others — making the transition smoother for organizations operating across borders.
